The same week Colorado signed the Colorado Artist Company Act into law — the first US legal entity specifically designed for artists. Three locked defaults: 51% artist voting control in statute (can't be overridden by the operating agreement, unlike a regular LLC), IP reversion on dissolution, economic rights split from governance. Yancey Strickler and the Artist Corporations Foundation pushed it for two years. 5,000 artists signed up to form one before it was even law. First filings open July 2027. ZAO's entire incubator thesis — artists keep ownership, outsiders fund without controlling — now exists as a statutory primitive. We're not forming one yet. We're in line.

Rodo mentioned he set up an LLC in Prospera (a special-jurisdiction zone) because a Mexican NGO can't legally hold a token — not even a governance token, or Bank of Mexico gets involved. The problem he's solving and the problem the Colorado A-Corp solves are the same problem: there is no legal structure that does what a creator-owned organization actually needs to do. The workarounds keep multiplying — Delaware LLCs, Cayman foundations, Prospera jurisdiction, Wyoming DAOs — until last week, when one state finally wrote the real version into law. That's a real milestone, and the ZAO is aligned with it by design.
