## Findings

### 1. Surety bond and general liability insurance are different requirements, not the same line item

Doc 1032 already found City of Ellsworth Ordinance Chapter 14 requires a corporate surety bond for the mass-gathering permit. This doc confirms the two are structurally different: a surety bond protects the city against ZAOstock's failure to meet its obligations (cleanup, damages, compliance) and is typically issued by a bonding company, while general liability insurance protects against attendee injury or property-damage claims and is issued by an insurance carrier. Ellsworth's published ordinance doesn't state a specific bond dollar amount - that needs a direct check with the City when filing. No source found gives a bond cost, since it depends on the amount the city sets. [FULL - per subagent research, cross-referenced against doc 1032's existing ordinance finding]

### 2. Realistic cost: $300-$600 - a real, meaningful chunk of the actual budget, not a rounding error

Multiple event-insurance providers quote entry-level one-day festival policies in the low hundreds of dollars, and a nonprofit-event benchmark study found a $182 median cost for comparable one-day events. For a 500-person outdoor festival with vendors and a stage, $300-$600 is a realistic planning number for general liability alone. **Correction 2026-07-12:** doc 1013's original comparison against a $20K budget (making this feel like a trivial line item inside a $2,000 contingency line) was wrong - the real ZAOstock budget is ~$5K target with only ~$1.5K on hand. Against that real number, $300-$600 for insurance alone is a genuinely significant planning constraint, not background noise. [FULL - per subagent research, multiple provider quotes cross-checked; budget comparison corrected 2026-07-12]

### 3. The fiscal sponsor almost certainly doesn't cover this - confirm, don't assume

ZAOstock's public/tax-deductible funding path runs through Fractured Atlas and New Media Commons (per the live `/sponsor` page). Fractured Atlas's own insurance program for sponsored projects ended December 31, 2019, and isn't part of current fiscal-sponsorship terms - liability responsibility sits with the sponsored project under their standard model. No public documentation was found on whether New Media Commons or ENTERACT (the commercial-sponsor path) include any event-liability coverage. **This is the one finding in this doc that needs a direct confirmation, not just research** - the safe assumption is that ZAOstock needs to arrange its own policy, but the actual answer should come from a direct question to those organizations, not from this doc's general research. [PARTIAL - Fractured Atlas's own published insurance-program end date is FULL; New Media Commons/ENTERACT's specific terms were not found and need direct confirmation]

### 4. Vendors and performers typically carry their own coverage - worth requiring proof, not assuming

Standard event practice is that food vendors and performers carry their own liability insurance and provide a certificate of insurance naming the event organizer as additional insured. This connects directly to doc 1044's food-vendor plan - the vendor confirmation checklist that doc recommends should include an insurance-status question, not just permit status. [FULL - per subagent research, cross-referenced against doc 1044's vendor sourcing plan]

### 5. Alcohol is a real open question that materially changes the insurance picture

Nowhere in ZAOstock's research - across every doc this session touched, including the day-of operations plan - is there a stated answer on whether alcohol will be served or sold at the festival. If it will be, liquor liability coverage is a distinct, separate requirement that can meaningfully raise the total insurance cost (sourced guidance suggests 2-3x the base policy). This needs a direct answer before any insurance quote can be treated as final - it's not a research gap, it's a decision nobody has made yet. [FULL - directly observed absence across this session's own research corpus]

### 6. "Additional insured" status is standard practice elsewhere in Maine, likely expected here too

Ellsworth's own published ordinance text doesn't explicitly state an "additional insured" requirement, but comparable Maine municipal event-permit practice (Westbrook, Portland examples found this session) treats it as standard. The safe move is to ask directly when filing the Aug 19 permit application, rather than assume either way. The same question applies to Heart of Ellsworth as the venue party - no public documentation exists on their specific insurance requirements for parklet use. [PARTIAL - general Maine municipal practice is FULL-sourced; Ellsworth's and HoE's specific requirement is not confirmed]
