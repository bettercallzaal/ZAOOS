> resources beyond the already-known targets (Livepeer, Coop Records, Greenpill). Honest finding up

> still-open leads survive the check.

## Findings

### 1. Most Maine grant deadlines for 2026 have already passed - a sobering, useful finding

Checked against today's date (2026-07-13): Levitt Foundation Festival Grants ($5,000, deadline Jan 30 -
passed), Maine Arts Commission Creative Communities Grant ($7,000, deadline March 30 - passed, and
explicitly does NOT accept fiscally-sponsored non-501(c)(3) applicants anyway), NEFA's FY26 rounds (Dec 1
2025 and April 1 2026 - both passed), Maine Community Foundation's Community Building Grant (deadline
February 2026 - passed), and Bangor Savings Bank Foundation's lower tier ($2K-5K, deadlines Jan 1/July 1 -
July 1 just passed, next is Jan 1 2027). **Three still-open exceptions carry this doc's real value:**
Maine Expansion Arts (already a known lead, ~September 2026 deadline, still open), the Libra Foundation
($25K max, Aug 15 2026 deadline for a September board meeting - genuinely new and open), and Bangor
Savings Bank Foundation's upper tier ($5,001-25,000, deadline October 1 2026 - open, but a 45-75 day
decision window means funds likely land after Oct 3, useful for reimbursement or 2027 rather than
pre-event cash). [FULL - 11 Maine/New England grant programs checked directly against current dates]

### 2. Free AV gear doesn't really exist - discounts and in-kind trades are the real ceiling

No verified source of genuinely free livestream production hardware (a switcher, audio interface) was
found for a small out-of-state nonprofit. What is real: **B&H Photo's nonprofit B2B program** (register
at their nonprofit portal, requires 501(c)(3) status and a nonprofit email domain - not gmail/yahoo - for
roughly 10-20% off, not free), and **Portland Media Center's 25% nonprofit-membership discount on studio
rentals** (Portland, ~2 hours from Ellsworth). Dead ends worth knowing about so nobody re-checks them:
TechSoup (software/cloud only, zero AV hardware), University of Maine's Fogler Library equipment loans
(students/faculty only, community orgs ineligible), Prince George's County library system near ZAOville
(personal borrowing only, no organizations). Two real unknowns worth a phone call rather than more
searching: the Maine State Library's media-equipment loan catalog (207-287-5600, has some AV gear but no
confirmed switcher/interface, and nonprofit eligibility itself is unconfirmed) and UMA Ellsworth (right in
town, unclear if they lend to community nonprofits - worth asking directly). [FULL - 9 sources checked,
Maine and Maryland both]

### 3. PEG (public access TV) is the one lead that could eliminate the gear cost entirely, not just discount it

Community Television Association of Maine (CTAM, ctamaine.org) runs the network of PEG access channels
across the state. The pattern worth checking: some PEG stations will send a volunteer crew with their own
production gear to cover a community event for free, in exchange for the right to air the footage on
their public-access channel - which would solve the ENTIRE livestream gear question doc 1030/1035 have
been carrying, not just make it cheaper. The catch: Ellsworth's specific PEG contact on file is stale
(a 2004-era name), so this needs a live call to CTAM to find out if Ellsworth still has active PEG access
and whether this kind of arrangement is realistic - it's a real lead, not a confirmed win. [FULL - checked
CTAM's own site and cross-referenced against Ellsworth's specific PEG status, which is unconfirmed]

### 4. Web3/crypto funding for the physical festival itself is a real dead end - redirect the effort

Checked Base ecosystem grants (Base Batches, Builder Hubs, Get Funded docs), Optimism RetroPGF, Gitcoin
Grants, Polygon Community Grants, and Arbitrum's grant programs. None fund physical/IRL events - they're
structured for shipped software, protocol work, and developer tooling. The one exception with real
IRL-event framing (Arbitrum's Gitcoin-Stack community-events matching pool, $12.5K ARB) is
Arbitrum-specific, not Base, and is a matching-fund model requiring base donations first - low fit. The
one genuinely live and relevant lead: **Livepeer's Event Marketing Fund**, currently in pre-proposal
stage on Livepeer's own forum, explicitly designed to put Livepeer's livestream infrastructure at
"high-profile Web3 events" in exchange for brand visibility - this is an equipment/services trade, not a
cash grant, and the way in is a direct conversation with Livepeer, not a public form. Also worth noting:
the crypto-native music/artist landscape has contracted sharply since 2021-2022 (Sound.xyz pivoted off
NFT-first, Audius token down ~95% from peak) - don't expect marquee crypto-native talent, but genuine
indie artists with engaged onchain fanbases willing to trade a slot for exposure + WaveWarZ-style payout
still exist. [FULL - 15+ sources checked across Base, Optimism, Gitcoin, Polygon, Arbitrum, and the
music-NFT space specifically]
