# 637 - Emily Sundberg "Open Tab" - Feed Me as solo media business

> Goal: extract operator playbook applicable to ZAO newsletter + community monetization.

## TL;DR

Emily Sundberg built Feed Me (10,000+ paid subscribers, 150,000+ total readers) solo on Substack by starting behind a paywall, hiring for diversity-of-perspective over credentials, and expanding into a studio ecosystem (podcast, job board, events) rather than a single product. Core lesson: scarcity + community comments + studio approach = stickiness.

## Who is Emily Sundberg + Feed Me

Emily Sundberg launched Feed Me in 2022, a daily business/tech/culture newsletter. She draws from prior roles at Meta, New York Magazine, and DTC companies. Currently 10,000+ paid subscribers, ~150,000 total readers across 250 annual sends. Recently hired managing editor and associate editor. Expanding West Coast edition with monthly coverage + quarterly in-person visits.

## Key takeaways (5)

1. **Start behind a paywall, not in front.** Launched Feed Me directly as a paid product despite having zero initial audience. Creates scarcity, filters for committed readers, and builds a protected space for unfiltered commentary. Paywall became her moat.

2. **Paywall spaces generate exceptional engagement.** Free newsletters fight noise. Her paid subscriber comments section is so lively and safe she's "never scared that anything bad is happening." This is a retention flywheel: good community keeps people paid.

3. **Diversify into studio model, not just newsletter.** Podcast (Expense Account, Substack-sponsored), job board, merch, events, interview series. Each extension deepens value for subscribers and creates new revenue streams without fragmenting the core brand.

4. **Hire for unique perspective, not pedigree.** Actively recruits non-journalists (like J Lee, a podcast host who "wasn't a writer by background"). Diversity of experience > formal credentials. Creates fresh voices that keep core readers engaged.

5. **Outsource operations, own editorial.** By staying on Substack ("operations are dealt with"), she avoids backend overhead and dedicates 100% of energy to content quality. Platform choice is strategic.

## How this applies to ZAO newsletter (Year of the ZABAL)

- **Paywall or gated tiers:** Year of the ZABAL is currently free. Emily's success suggests testing a paid tier for deeper analysis or exclusive community access. Paragraph supports both free + paid micros, creating a funnel.

- **Community as product:** Her comment section is part of the stickiness. ZAO newsletter needs the same. Farcaster threading (public) or Paragraph's subscriber comments (gated) can replace email comments.

- **Studio expansion:** ZAO newsletter has adjacent products (podcast, events, artist interviews, drops). Link them explicitly. Emily's job board + podcast aren't separate brands; they're extensions of Feed Me. Same for ZAO's music releases, spaces, and governance.

- **Non-credentialed voices:** ZAO's strength is community members writing about their own work, not hired journalists. Lean into this. Feature artist interviews, member economy analysis, fractal process deep-dives from participants.

## What Zaal should steal (tactics)

1. **Test a paid tier on Paragraph** by end of Q2 2026. Free newsletter builds audience (2-3 months), then unlock 1-2 weekly posts for $15/mo or comparable. Paragraph Coins + x402 micropaywalls already set up (doc 429).

2. **Revive the comment section** as a deliberate community feature. Right now Year of the ZABAL is broadcast-only. Add a Farcaster channel or Paragraph subscriber comments for reader discussion. Emily's success proves engaged communities stay longer.

3. **Hire one freelancer** (not staffer) by Q2 who covers a beat Emily doesn't cover. Suggestion: artist economy trends, web3 music moves, or ZAO community updates. Get perspective from outside leadership.
