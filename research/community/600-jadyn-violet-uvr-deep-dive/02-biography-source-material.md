## The Arc, in One Paragraph

An academically gifted son of Indian immigrants is pushed toward a "safe" business degree, falls in love with making music in his first year of college, watches his grades collapse, and drops out in his third semester against intense family resistance. Two dark, broke, isolated years follow in central New Jersey, where he teaches himself production in his bedroom. In late 2021 he discovers on-chain music — and for the first time finds the thing web2 never gave him: real community. He turns a daily Twitter Space into a movement, throws raves in city after city, hand-draws 1,800 NFT characters, raises tens of ETH, and builds a brand on the bet that kindness and consistency compound. Then the market he bet on collapses: the NFT winter sets in, his flagship collection stalls at 38% minted, the album he promised never ships, the raves go quiet, and his distribution platform (Sound.xyz) shuts down entirely. He pivots to daily Twitch streaming. The biography's central tension: a genuinely talented community-builder whose conviction was real but whose timing and chosen rails were not — and what he does next.

## Chronological Narrative

### Chapter 1 — The overachiever (childhood -> ~2019)

Jadyn grows up in central New Jersey, first-generation Indian American, in a household where his father is a lifelong professional singer. Music and performance are in the air from the start — his earliest musical memory is Michael Jackson, and at family functions he would dress as MJ and dance to "Billie Jean." But the family's expectation for *him* is the immigrant-household script: excel academically, get the secure career. He delivers — near-perfect SAT, top student — and enrolls at Rutgers to study business. It is, in his own framing, aimless: doing what he was told without knowing what he wanted.

### Chapter 2 — The break (~2019 -> 2020)

In his first year at Rutgers, making music stops being a far-off dream and becomes urgent. A breakup is the catalyst — he writes his first song as catharsis and immediately knows: "I want to be a musician." By his second year, music has taken over. His grades collapse — "from perfect scores to almost failing all my courses." His mother is "super disappointed, believing that I had given up in life." In his third semester he drops out. For a first-gen Indian American household, this is, in his words, "the worst fear for any Indian parents." He gets significant backlash and spends months defending the decision: he could accomplish great things in anything he fully committed to, and that it was not school did not mean he had given up.

### Chapter 3 — The dark years (2020 -> 2021)

He calls the next two years "a dark and scary journey." Broke, isolated in central New Jersey far from any music scene, working odd jobs to survive, teaching himself production and songwriting in his bedroom. He tries to build an audience on Instagram and TikTok — gets videos that "blow up" but nothing sustains past a month. He describes web2 music relationships as "surface level," "egotistical and narcissistic." He wants community that connects "deeper than a fire emoji or a repost." He also resells sneakers on StockX in this period — an early, hollow brush with marketplace entrepreneurship that taught mechanics but no meaning.

### Chapter 4 — Finding web3 and finding community (late 2021 -> early 2022)

In late 2021 he discovers on-chain music and music NFTs, right as the technology becomes viable. He frames it explicitly as a second chance — he had "missed" earlier waves (he likes to say he passed on minting a Bored Ape for under $1,000) and was determined not to be late again. Within about five months he experiences something genuinely new: "Community was a term I never understood because it wasn't always present in my life. I now have a group of creatives surrounding me that actually celebrate me for me." This is the hinge of the whole story — the emotional payoff that everything after is built to protect and scale.

### Chapter 5 — Building UVR (2021 -> 2023)

It starts as a daily Twitter Space — "Violet Rave," soon "Underground Violet Rave." Originally a 10pm-5am marathon, later moved to 11am EST for reach. Not transactional: panels, music-NFT talk, philosophy, "literally anything," POAP "Orbs" for showing up. He hosts it every single day. Someone in the community suggests he throw an in-person rave; he thinks it is "absolutely crazy" and does it anyway. The first UVR — Brooklyn, NFT NYC, September 2022 — draws 250+ people from around the world, the same people who had been voices in the Space. That is the realization: "This is the community that I've always wanted to build, and it's happening right in front of my eyes." He goes "all out": raves in Virginia, Cincinnati, Miami (500+ at Art Basel), Denver, LA, NYC. By late 2023: 300+ Spaces, 7+ raves across 6 cities. In parallel he ships music NFTs (Violet Token April 2022; "Good Always Turns Evil" sells out for 1.75 ETH).

### Chapter 6 — Raver Realm and the bet (October 2023)

The centerpiece: Raver Realm, "The Music Collectors PFP." 1,800 hand-drawn characters — he draws every one himself in Paint.NET on a Wacom tablet — across 3 "Realms" (Underground, Dystopian, Ethereal), each character paired to one of 9 songs he selected from 100+ written over a year. The 9 songs are meant to roll up, "Good Fridays"-style, into his debut album, "Violet." Tiered mint (free for holders up through 0.03 ETH public). It is the synthesis of everything — music, art, community, IRL, lore — into one ownable thing. It is also launched directly into the music-NFT winter.

### Chapter 7 — The winter and the pivot (2024 -> 2026)

The bet does not pay the way the model needed. Raver Realm stalls around 38% minted (~679 of 1,800). The rave series goes dormant after late 2023. No new music NFT drops follow. The "Violet" album — promised for "early June 2023" — never ships as an album, though the 9 songs trickle out as DSP singles through 2024-2025. In January 2025 he pivots: daily Twitch streaming, "Just Chatting" — community hangs, song-battle games, co-streams — not music production. He leaves New Jersey and moves to Los Angeles "with no money." He commits to a 365-day streak and completes it — then releases "The 365 Documentary: One Year Can Change Your Life" (YouTube, Jan 30, 2026) about it. He grows fast (zero to ~2,000 followers in under four months), peaks at 7,472 concurrent viewers (July 2025), plateaus around ~9,700 followers / ~616 subs. In October 2025 he throws a UVR show at TwitchCon San Diego (~438 attendees) — the 9th UVR event and the first tied to the Twitch era. In November 2025 he moves into a shipping-container studio in LA, "The Great Upgrade." By May 2026 his distribution platform Sound.xyz has shut down entirely, and the daily Twitch cadence appears to have broken (trackers disagree). The story is open-ended: a real builder, mid-reinvention, the next chapter unwritten.

## Themes for the Biography

A biographer has at least six strong throughlines to choose from or weave:

1. **The first-gen rebellion.** The whole story rhymes off one decision — dropping out against an immigrant family's deepest fear — and the lifelong project of proving it was right. Every venture is, in part, a justification of Chapter 2.
2. **Community as the thing he never had.** He is explicit that "community" was absent from his life until web3. UVR is not a growth tactic; it is the emotional payload. This is the warmest, most human read of the story.
3. **Kindness as a business thesis.** "Being kind never got me far in web2, but in web3 it's one of the main things pushing me forward." He bet a career on the idea that generosity and consistency compound. The biography can test that thesis against the outcome.
4. **The artist who became an entrepreneur to survive — see the entrepreneurial thread below.**
5. **Right conviction, wrong timing.** He was genuinely early and genuinely committed, and he still launched his flagship into a collapsing market. A study in how much timing taxes even real talent.
6. **The permanent reinvention.** Bedroom musician -> Spaces host -> rave promoter -> NFT artist -> Twitch streamer. The constant is daily, public, founder-fronted output; the platform keeps changing under him.

## The Entrepreneurial Thread (specifically)

The user's goal names "artistic AND entrepreneurial." The entrepreneurial story is distinct and traceable:

- **Earliest instinct — sneaker reselling on StockX** (the dark years). Marketplace mechanics, arbitrage, no meaning. The "before" picture.
- **The pivot insight:** he reframes being a musician as being a *founder*. Web3 is not where he sells songs — it is where he can "build a foundational brand" and "capitalize on being early," the thing he felt he missed with SoundCloud and TikTok.
- **Productizing membership — Violet Token (2022).** Not a song. A community membership / access pass, with public tiered goals. His first real product.
- **The funnel — Spaces -> Orbs -> raves -> NFT.** A coherent, free-at-the-top, paid-deeper acquisition funnel built by hand, daily, for years.
- **Vertical integration — Raver Realm.** He is the musician, the illustrator (1,800 hand-drawn pieces), the community manager, the event promoter, and the brand. Almost no outsourcing. A strength (authenticity, margin) and a fatal constraint (it does not scale past one person's hours).
- **The IRL bet — the rave series as a brand.** Treating live events as a repeatable, multi-city product, not one-offs.
- **The hard lesson — single-platform risk.** Everything routed through Sound.xyz and Twitter Spaces. When Spaces lost reach and Sound.xyz shut down, the business had no second rail. Contrast peers (Daniel Allan, Reo Cragun) who deliberately stayed hybrid.
- **The reinvention — Twitch as the next rail.** Whether this is adaptation or retreat is the open question the biography has to sit with honestly.

The entrepreneurial verdict from the research: **musician-first, entrepreneur-by-necessity.** He built the company because that was the only way the art reached anyone. That framing — art as the why, enterprise as the how — is probably the spine of the book.

## Open Questions Only Jadyn Can Answer (interview targets)

A biography needs primary-source interviews. The research leaves these genuinely open:

1. **Why Twitch, and why then?** No public statement explains the January 2025 pivot. Burnout on Spaces? Money? Discovery? This is the pivotal unexplained decision.
2. **What happened to the "Violet" album?** Promised June 2023, never shipped as an album. Abandoned, deferred, or redefined?
3. **What did the dark years actually look like day to day?** The narrative is told in summary ("dark and scary"). The texture is missing.
4. **Where is the family now?** The arc opens with family conflict. Did the relationship heal? Has the bet been vindicated in their eyes?
5. **Is he financially sustainable?** ~17 ETH verified revenue plus unverified amounts, against years of full-time work, 1,800 hand-drawn pieces, and cross-country events. The Taco Bell franchise dream (a recurring half-joke) hints he thinks about this.
6. **How does he feel about the NFT winter?** He launched into it. Does he regret the timing, the rails, or nothing?
7. **What is UVR now?** Dormant, paused, or quietly continuing? Only he can say whether the rave series and Raver Realm are over or resting.
8. **The romantic thread.** "Under The Same Stars" is about a 3-year separation from "someone who meant the world to me." A biography may or may not go here — his call, and Zaal's editorial call.
