## What is an Impact Network? (Ehrlichman's Framework)

An impact network is **a living system of people, organizations, and resources deliberately organized around shared purpose to coordinate actions across complex problems**. Unlike traditional coalitions or collaborations, which are transaction-based, impact networks are **relationship-based and generative** — they create value from the connections themselves, not just from individual contributions.

Key differences from related forms:

- **vs. Coalition**: Coalitions are temporary alliances around a single issue. Impact networks are persistent, multi-issue, and operate as emergent systems.
- **vs. Collaboration**: Collaboration is often bilateral (two orgs working together). Networks are multi-nodal and assume no single org can solve the problem.
- **vs. DAO**: DAOs encode rules in code; impact networks encode rules in relationships + shared purpose + trust. Hats Protocol bridges this gap by adding web3 permissions to relationship-based networks.
- **vs. Community**: Communities may lack intentional coordination or shared problem-solving focus. Impact networks are purpose-driven and action-coordinated.

Ehrlichman draws on network science, systems thinking, and 15+ years coordinating 60+ real networks globally (Santa Cruz Mountains Stewardship, Sustainable Conservation, PlantRight, Dreams InDeed, etc.).

## The 5 Core Principles + The 5 Steps (Living Process)

### 5 Network Principles (Non-Negotiable)

These principles must be "baked in" as networks emerge. They are structural, not aspirational:

1. **Trust, not control** - Authority comes from earned relationships, not hierarchy. No single org owns or dominates decisions.
2. **Humility, not brand** - Individual orgs/people fade into the background. Focus stays on the shared mission.
3. **Node, not hub** - Every participant is a peer node. Network is decentralized. No bottleneck at the center.
4. **Mission, not organization** - Success is measured by progress on the shared problem, not by the growth of any single org.
5. **Generous collaboration** - Assumption of positive intent. Look for opportunities to collaborate for mission, not personal/org gain. No quid pro quo.

### 5 Steps (Reaffirmed Continuously, Not Linear)

These aren't one-time tasks. Network leaders must cycle through them repeatedly as context changes and new people join:

1. **Clarify purpose** - Why does this network exist? What problem are we solving? Requires sense-making, mapping the system, identifying shared values, understanding local context + power dynamics.
2. **Convene the right people** - Must collectively represent all parts of the system, have ability to get things done, and be willing to cross boundaries. Includes even people you'd prefer not to work with.
3. **Cultivate trust** - Build authentic relationships. Intentional, not passive. Must be "trust for impact" - grounded in shared purpose + shared values + clear behavioral norms when disagreements arise.
4. **Coordinate actions** - Leverage existing work, avoid duplication. Assign network roles (core team, facilitator, network manager). Don't require unanimity on all issues.
5. **Collaborate generously** - Share resources, knowledge, credit. Operate as successful givers (Adam Grant research). Focus on return on relationships.

## Hats Protocol Connection (Verified)

**CONFIRMED: David Ehrlichman is a co-founder of Hats Protocol alongside Spencer Graham and Nick Naraghi.**

Ehrlichman moved into web3 after realizing that traditional nonprofits face perverse incentives to NOT collaborate (funders want attribution of direct causality, so orgs silo). He saw web3's trustware (smart contracts) as a solution to encode collaboration rules automatically.

Key insight from Ehrlichman's work with Hats:

> "Smart contracts enable creating constraints pre-programmed and encoded into the rules of the system... You can have proactive accountability and dynamic accountability... [Hats enables] delegating signing authority with the ability to revoke it as well."

**The bridge**: Hats Protocol solves the "Dunbar's number problem" - impact networks lose effectiveness past 150 people because synchronous trust-building becomes difficult. Hats adds **on-chain role clarity and permission delegation**, allowing networks to scale past that limit while maintaining accountability.

**For The ZAO specifically**: Hats could formalize node roles (project leads, governance, treasury) without requiring centralized control. Each ZAO Project could be a Hats tree with delegated signing authority. Federated, not hierarchical.

## 5-10 Concrete Actions for Zaal

### Phase 1: Foundation (Weeks 1-4)

1. **Write The ZAO Impact Network Charter** (2 pages max)
   - Codify the 5 principles with ZAO-specific language.
   - Example: "Node, not hub" becomes "Each ZAO Project operates as an autonomous node; Zaal and Founding Council facilitate, not control."
   - Lock shared purpose statement. Use Doc 547 language as foundation.
   - Share publicly on zaoos.com/network or zao-principles.md.

2. **Map the full system** (with Research team)
   - Plot 40 members + their existing work (music, tech, organizing, art, etc.).
   - Identify gaps (missing perspectives, underrepresented sectors).
   - Find leverage points (e.g., who can bridge to City of Ellsworth? Who brings nonprofit expertise?).
   - Output: Confluence diagram + 1-pager.

3. **Establish synchronous trust cadence**
   - Keep Fractal Mondays (locked).
   - ADD: Monthly "all-hands" open call (90 min, rotating time zones if possible).
   - ADD: Quarterly in-person gatherings (tie to ZAOstock sprint weekends, Ellsworth events).
   - Goal: Scale trust past 20 people to 100+.

### Phase 2: Structure (Weeks 5-8)

4. **Formalize node roles with Hats Protocol**
   - Define roles: Project Lead, Governance, Treasury, Community, Research, Operations.
   - Deploy Hats tree on Base (where ZAO lives).
   - Assign hats to current leads (WaveWarZ, ZAOstock, ZOE, Bonfire, etc.).
   - Use Hats to gate signing authority on shared treasury (when created).
   - Resources: Hats docs at github.com/Hats-Protocol, Ehrlichman's Decential article.

5. **Create federation agreement template**
   - Codify how ZAO Projects relate to The ZAO umbrella.
   - Template: Each project = autonomous LLC or cooperative, retains IP + decision-making.
   - ZAO = facilitator, not owner. Provides: network access, brand, shared services (legal, ops, research).
   - Projects commit to: sharing learnings, hiring from ZAO community first, generosity (e.g., teaching other projects).
   - Gets signed by WaveWarZ, ZAOstock, any future projects.

6. **Establish resource pooling mechanism**
   - ZAO has no treasury yet. Propose lightweight model:
     - Shared fund for network activities (convenings, research, infrastructure).
     - Funded by ZAO Festivals / passive revenue (if any).
     - Managed by rotating treasurer (not Zaal).
     - Decisions by consensus or Hats delegation.
   - Start with $5K / quarter allocation to test.

### Phase 3: Emergence (Weeks 9-12)

7. **Decommission hub-and-spoke, celebrate nodes**
   - Reframe comms: "Zaal founded The ZAO. Now it's powered by its members."
   - Highlight project leaders in socials, website, pitches (not just Zaal).
   - Give project leads speaking slots at Fractal Mondays + ZAO events.
   - Example: "Hurric4n3ike leads WaveWarZ. Cassie stewards ZAOstock. Spencer owns Bonfire strategy."

8. **Establish cross-project coordination**
   - Monthly standup: 15 min async (Slack doc) or 30 min sync call.
   - Agenda: What's shipped? What's blocked? Who can help?
   - Prevents siloing. Surfaces opportunities for collaboration (e.g., ZAOstock uses WaveWarZ music; Bonfire indexes both).

9. **Document network health quarterly**
   - Measure: Trust (pulse survey), Convening frequency, Coordination velocity, Generosity (resource sharing ratio).
   - Publish 1-pager each quarter (part of research docs).
   - Adjust cadence + structure based on data.

10. **Plan the federation expansion**
    - As new projects pitch (e.g., AI Taste, Respect, ZID - from future-repos.md), apply the charter + federation template.
    - Make it easy for founders to join as nodes, hard to stay as spokes.
    - Goal: By Dec 2026, 5-7 projects operating as coordinated federation, not Zaal-led org.

## International Expansion - Operationalizing the 2040 Goal's Second Half

The May 2026 pass of this doc scored ZAO's internal readiness (2.4/5) but never addressed the international, capacity-building half of Zaal's 2040 goal. Fresh research (2026-07-10) into Converge's own client history shows this isn't speculative - Ehrlichman's organization has done exactly this kind of work, repeatedly, in under-resourced and cross-border contexts:

| Network | Region | What Converge Actually Did |
|---------|--------|----------------------------|
| Puerto Rico Impact Investment Network | Puerto Rico | Lead partner from concept through year one - network cultivation, convening design/facilitation, interim coordination, catalyzing investors + business leaders + philanthropies + community orgs around a resilient local economy |
| Asia Pacific Refugee Rights Network (APRRN) | Asia-Pacific | Advised the Secretary General/Secretariat, facilitated team learning sessions, wrote the governance plan, ran network-health assessment + social network analysis training - a 7+ year live network (independently corroborated: [ReliefWeb](https://reliefweb.int/organization/aprrn), [Devex](https://www.devex.com/organizations/asia-pacific-refugee-rights-network-73693), a [ResearchGate case study "Growing Pains: APRRN at Seven Years"](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/305648186_Growing_Pains_The_Asia_Pacific_Refugee_Rights_Network_at_Seven_Years)) |
| Women in Africa Investment Network | Africa | Advised the network catalyst/coordinator on the network's health and evolution |
| International Trade Centre's Alliances for Action | Multi-regional (SDG/trade-focused) | Introduced network concepts, co-created theory of change, designed coordination-hub org structures, launched pilot networks, built assessment tools |
| Partners Network | Latin America, Middle East, Africa, Eastern Europe, US | Internal-learning network among country offices of global civil-society peacebuilding orgs |

**What this means operationally for The ZAO:** none of these were "Converge opens an office in the region." Every single case is the same pattern - go find people/orgs already doing the work locally, then provide convening design, interim coordination, governance-plan co-design, and network-health tooling until a local coordinator can run it themselves. That maps directly onto Ehrlichman's "node, not hub" principle (already scored 2/5 for ZAO's *internal* structure above) - the international expansion should follow the identical logic, not a separate playbook. For a "place that doesn't have resources," the ZAO's actual export isn't capital or a branch office - it's the network-cultivation methodology itself, plus access to the existing ZAO node network (WaveWarZ artist pipeline, ZABAL Games mentorship structure, Respect/Hats permissioning) as the connective tissue.

**One claim flagged, not confirmed:** an initial web search characterized Ehrlichman's approach as "scale out, not scale up" (cooperation vs. centralized growth). A direct fetch of the primary SSIR excerpt could not verify this as an actual Ehrlichman quote or phrase from that source - it may be a secondary-source paraphrase. The underlying pattern (network cultivation + interim coordination + local handoff, evidenced by the 5 case studies above) is independently confirmed regardless of whether this specific phrase is authentically Ehrlichman's; don't cite "scale out not scale up" as a direct quote without finding it in the actual book text.

**Concrete implication for The ZAO Protocol capstone paper:** the 2040 goal's global-expansion half should be framed as "export the network-cultivation methodology + connect local creator networks into the ZAO graph," not "ZAO opens chapters." This is a materially different (and more credible) claim than an unstructured "help build them" - it has 5 real precedents to cite.

## Complementary Frameworks Worth Reading

1. **Sociocracy & Holacracy** - Consent-based governance and self-organizing team structures. Sociocracy emphasizes psychological safety; Holacracy distributes authority via roles. Both can work inside impact networks as operational mechanisms. Start: [Sociocracy For All](https://www.sociocracyforall.org/).

2. **Exit to Community (Nathan Schneider)** - When startups/platforms transition to member ownership. Directly applicable to ZAO: how do founders hand power to community while maintaining vision? See also: Polis, Arweave, Gitcoin. Takeaway: *Ownership structure reflects power structure. Design both intentionally.*

3. **Network Federalism (ConstitutionDAO retrospectives)** - Multi-org networks can use federated governance (each node votes) instead of centralized voting. Avoids whale capture. Ehrlichman doesn't prescribe this, but it pairs well with impact networks. Takeaway: *Use federation as circuit-breaker against power concentration.*

4. **Teal Organizations (Frederic Laloux)** - Self-managed, purpose-driven orgs with no hierarchy. Overlaps with impact networks but focuses on single org. Hats Protocol is the web3 version. Takeaway: *Decentralized decision-making requires clear roles, not less structure.*

5. **Network Weaver Handbook (June Holley)** - Complements Ehrlichman. Focuses on the *people* side: conflict resolution, relationship-building in networks. Ehrlichman is systems + principles; Holley is people + skills. Takeaway: *Networks need both architects and weavers.*

## Summary for Zaal

The ZAO has the DNA of an impact network - shared purpose, trust within core group, willingness to incubate community projects. But it's not *structured* as one yet. Ehrlichman's framework shows that this requires:

1. **Written principles** (node/hub, mission/org, trust, humility, generosity).
2. **Synchronous trust-building** (monthly calls, quarterly in-person).
3. **Federated authority** (projects as equal nodes, Zaal as facilitator).
4. **Smart contracts** (Hats Protocol for permissioning, accountability).
5. **Shared resources** (treasury, research, legal, ops - pooled, not hoarded).

The good news: You have buy-in from cofounders (WaveWarZ, ZAOstock teams). You have an ethos of generosity. You have a clear problem to solve (supporting creators). You have 15+ months of proof (Fractal Mondays running consistently).

The work: Documenting + operationalizing the principles. Making the hub-to-federation shift explicit and irreversible. Using Hats to scale trust technologically past the founder.

Zaal, consider this a 12-week sprint. By August 2026, The ZAO should be legible to outsiders as a true impact network - not a music community around one founder, but a federation of creator projects supporting each other for systemic change.
