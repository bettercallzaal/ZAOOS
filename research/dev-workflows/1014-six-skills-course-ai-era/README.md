# 1014 - Six Durable Skills for the AI Era (Zaal's Self-Paced Course)

## The Thesis

> "Learn AI" is generic and backwards. Instead, focus on the six specific skill sets that become MORE valuable as AI gets better, not less. Each skill is learnable this weekend and maps directly to ZAO's architecture + your personal brand + agentic loops. You already have three of these in your bones. This course operationalizes all six.

**For Zaal:** You ship constantly but your distribution is often "build and tweet." You understand community and events deeply. You think in agents. This course wires distribution-first thinking, systematizes daily curation, and formalizes the feedback loops you already run intuitively. The result: fewer hours spent explaining what you built, more hours spent watching how people use it.

---

## How to Take This Course

This is not a lecture series. This is a bootcamp.

**The Discipline:**
- One module per week (six weeks total) or compress two modules into one week if you run hot.
- Each module has ONE shippable project due by Sunday of that week. That project ships in public (Telegram, X, Farcaster, Bonfire, the app itself—wherever your audience sees it).
- The metric for completion is not "I read it" but "I shipped it and measured what happened."
- Practice daily. Most modules have a 15-30 minute daily ritual.
- You will feel messy for the first 2-3 days of each module. Ship messy. The loop fixes it.

**Why This Works for You:**
You wake at 4:30am and build 4-7pm. You ship constantly. You think in rapid feedback loops (ZABAL Games, ZAOstock, ZOE iterations). This course aligns with that rhythm: small shippable chunks, daily practice, public iteration, measure and fold back into the next cycle.

**Blockers to Avoid:**
- "I don't want to ship something half-baked." Ship it. Half-baked feedback > perfect silence.
- "I need more time to understand this before I try it." Do the exercise first, then read the theory. Learning happens in the doing.
- "This feels too basic / I already know this." You know pieces. This course is about the full loop: concept to daily practice to measurement to the next cycle. Run the whole loop before you skip.

## How This Runs On Your Actual Morning

Your real morning is not one long build block. It is three different environments back to back, each good for a different kind of work. The course fails if it fights that; it works if it maps onto it. Here is the split and what each block is for.

**Wake 4:20 -> 30 minutes at the desk. This is the deep-work slot.** It is the sharpest cognitive window of your day, right after waking, before the world loads in. Spend it on the ONE hardest thinking task of the module, not on email or triage. The rule that makes this work: prep the task the night before, so at 4:20 you open one file and start cold, zero warmup, zero deciding-what-to-do. Writing, deciding, designing, the hard 20 hooks, the pain sentence - this is where they go.

**Shower / get ready. Reset, no work.** Let the desk task keep cooking in the background. Do not check a screen.

**45-minute car ride to work. This is AUDIO / VOICE mode - no coding, hands and eyes are driving.** This block is worth as much as the desk block, but only for the right kind of work: listening and talking. Listen to the module read aloud or a research doc; voice-draft casts and hooks into a memo; talk through a decision out loud and let the recording be the artifact; take the course itself by audio. You cannot type here, so never plan a typing task for the car. The car is where you consume the input and rehearse the output; the desk is where you produce it.

**Gym 6-7. Physical recovery. Rituals go AFTER, not before.** Do not stack a cognitive ritual on top of the workout. If a module has a "reflect / measure" step, do it post-gym or fold it into the next morning's desk slot.

### The daily loop, mapped

The pattern every day: **car = input + rehearse, desk = produce, gym = recover.** You take in the module and voice-draft the raw material on today's drive; you sit down the next morning and ship the polished version in 30 focused minutes. Input and production are one day offset - that offset is a feature, it means you always sit down with raw material already in hand.

### Per-module fit

- **Week 1 (Distribution):** Desk = write the pain sentence + the 20 hooks (hardest thinking). Car = read the 20 hooks out loud, feel which ones land, voice-memo the rewrites. Ship the daily 60-second take from the desk; script it by voice on the drive first.
- **Week 2 (Curation):** Car = consume the niche (listen to the feeds/podcasts, voice-note what actually mattered). Desk = write the day's one-line point-of-view take. This module is almost made for the car.
- **Week 3 (Agents):** Desk only - this is build/config work that needs a keyboard. Car = listen to the module + talk through the agent's ONE job out loud so you sit down knowing exactly what to build.
- **Week 4 (48-hour ship loop):** Desk = build + read the DM feedback. Car = voice-draft the outreach DMs and the "what surprised me" note. Gym-after = the honest reflection on what to cut.
- **Week 5 (Events / recaps):** Car = voice-draft the recap and the follow-up messages (talking is the right medium for a recap anyway). Desk = turn the voice draft into the shipped recap doc + network asset.
- **Week 6 (Physical / atoms):** Car = observe + voice-note the physical-integration insights on the drive. Desk = write them into the product spec.

The through-line: **never plan a keyboard task for the car, never waste the 4:20 desk window on something you could have done by voice.** Match the task to the environment and the same 30+45 minutes does double the work.

## Module 1: Distribution Marketers (Week 1)

### The Skill in Two Sentences

You understand your audience (musicians, event organizers, music-tech builders, community enthusiasts). You know what they care about. The skill is finding the 20 places they already look for attention, translating what you built into their language, and testing which story resonates before you ship the final version.

### Why This Skill Is Durable

In an AI world, building products is easy. Everyone can ship a landing page or an app or a feature. The bottleneck is attention. Ten years from now, AI will be free and abundant. The person who wins is the one who figured out how to make people care. Distribution is not a channel (Twitter, Farcaster, TikTok). Distribution is a process: research the niche, write the pain sentence, test 20 hooks, keep the ones that work.

### Why It Matters for You and ZAO

You ship deeply. ZABAL Games, ZAOstock, ZOE, POIDH—all real problems solved in real ways. But distribution for each has been reactive: ship, then post about it. Zaal sees the magic and tweets. The gap: you are leaving 5x-10x the adoption on the table because you did not position the product where the niche was already looking. Distribution-first thinking means your next ship is already positioned before the code is done.

**Your Current State:** Zaal builds and tweets. BetterCallZaal has curator instinct. The gap: you are not systematically mapping where your audience lives, what language they use, and which stories move them. You think deeply but position reactively.

**The Multiplier:** If distribution-first iteration changes the way you scope features (adding onchain verification to ZABAL because tokens-in-web3 is the real anxiety, not just "I want to run a workshop"), you will ship things people actually pull instead of you needing to push.

### Module 1 Shippable Project

**Task: Distribution Map + 20 Hooks for Your Lab Product**

Pick your lab product from Day 1. Let's say it is ZABAL Games (new workshop format).

**Step 1 (Tuesday):** Write the Distribution Map. Answer these 20 questions about your audience (workshop organizers, music creators, event producers, music-tech builders—pick one persona):

```
Where does your audience already look for attention?

Newsletters they subscribe to:
Creators they follow:
Reddit communities they post in:
Slack groups they are in:
Podcasts they listen to:
YouTube channels they watch:
Events they attend:
Tools they already pay for:
Conferences they go to:
Communities they are part of:
Books/blogs they read:
Mentors they follow:
Peers they talk to:
Search terms they use (Google, Farcaster, X):
Competitors or adjacent products they use:
Discord / community groups they are in:
News sources they check:
Frameworks or philosophies they reference:
Influencers in this space:
Job boards or hiring places they check:
```

**Step 2 (Wednesday):** Write the Pain Sentence. This is one painful thing someone in your niche would actually say out loud. Not flowery. Actual words. Examples:

- "I run workshops but half the attendees are ghost when it comes time to actually learn, and I have no way to keep them engaged or measure what stuck."
- "I built a music feature but I have no idea if people actually want it or if they're just being polite."
- "Onchain events are cool but we have no way to reward people who showed up."

Write THREE pain sentences for your lab product. Test them with three people in that niche this week. Which one makes them nod and say "yes, that is the core problem"? That is your real pain sentence.

**Step 3 (Thursday/Friday):** Write 20 Hooks. Now you have one pain sentence. Write 20 different versions of the solution hook that rides that pain. Examples:

Curiosity hooks:
- "We figured out how to make workshops actually stick. One simple lever."
- "The metric that tells you if a workshop actually landed (hint: it's not attendance)."
- "I sat in 30 workshops last year. Here is the one pattern the best ones shared."

Fear hooks:
- "Most workshops are 90% waste. Here is how we changed that."
- "You're leaving retention on the table if you do X."

Status hooks:
- "The teams running workshops that scale all do this one thing differently."
- "The pattern the best event organizers just figured out."

Money hooks:
- "Workshops that use this method sell 3x more follow-on services."
- "This saves organizers 10 hours of follow-up per event."

Regret hooks:
- "I wish I knew this before I ran my first workshop."
- "This one insight would have changed how I built the first three programs."

Write 20 of these. Make them short (under 20 words each). Make 5 in each category. Write them in a note or doc. Do not polish. Ship them messy.

**Step 4 (Friday/Saturday):** Test 5 Hooks with 20 People. Pick your 5 best hooks (one from each category). Post them in 4 places where your niche hangs out: a Farcaster thread, an X thread, a Telegram group, or a DM chain with 5 collaborators. Do NOT post all at once. Post one hook on Monday, one on Tuesday, etc. Watch which one gets replies, screenshots, forwards, or actual engagement.

**Step 5 (Sunday):** Measure and Fold Back. Which hook got the most honest response? Not just likes—which one made someone actually engage or ask for more info? Use that hook as the baseline for your product's story. Write it into your launch copy, your demo, your conversation with the first 10 users.

**Sunday Deliverable:** One-page doc with: pain sentence (validated with 3 people), 20 hooks, test results (which 5 got tested, which resonated), and the winning hook + why you think it won.

### Daily Practice (Week 1)

- **Monday/Tuesday/Wednesday/Thursday/Friday:** One 20-minute session per day. Step 1 (pain sentence research) on Mon/Tue. Step 2 (write 20 hooks) on Wed/Thu. Step 3 (test and measure) on Fri.
- **Saturday/Sunday:** Analyze results. Write the one-pager.

This is not coding or design work. This is thinking + talking to people + listening.

2. **[BOOK]** "Traction" by Gabriel Weinberg. Chapter on distribution channels. Even though it's older, the framework of "which channels does your audience use" is timeless. Pick the 2-3 chapters that match your niche. Skim the rest.

3. **[NEWSLETTER]** "The Content Writer Newsletter" by Paul Millerd (newsletter.pmillerd.com). Every issue is about positioning one idea into 5 different formats. Subscribe for 2 weeks. Watch how he takes one insight (like "solo builders should focus on micro-audiences") and turns it into a tweet, a blog post, a video idea, a course framing, and a conversation angle. This is the skill.

4. **[TOOL]** Swipe file. Open a Google Doc or Notion called "Product Story - [Lab Product Name] - Swipe File". Every time you see a post, a headline, a tweet, a landing page, an email that makes you stop and re-read it, save it. Write why it worked (curiosity? fear? authority? surprise?). By week 3, you will have a library of what resonates in your niche.

### How You'll Know It Worked

- You sent 5 different hooks out into the world and got actual replies or engagement.
- You can point to one hook and say "this one made someone ask about the product before I even shipped it."
- You have a pain sentence that three people in your niche nodded at (not smiled politely, but nodded and said "yes").
- You rewrote your product description to lead with the winning hook instead of the feature list.

## Module 2: Curators/Yappers (Week 2)

### The Skill in Two Sentences

You watch your niche (AI for music, events, community, web3). Every day you spot one thing that matters and translate what it means for people like you. You yap about it in your own voice for 60 seconds. Over time, your audience trusts you because you consistently find the signal before they do.

### Why This Skill Is Durable

AI makes content abundant. Anyone can prompt an AI to write a newsletter or a thread. What gets scarce is authenticity. The person who curates what actually matters in a niche, in their own voice, becomes a filter people pay attention to. As AI gets better at writing, the person who has a point-of-view becomes more valuable, not less. Curation at scale becomes a moat.

### Why It Matters for You and ZAO

You think deeply about music + events + community + tech. BetterCallZaal is a curator account in spirit. The gap: you are thinking deeply but posting sparingly. You might post once a week or once every few days. The algorithm (on Farcaster, X, YouTube) rewards consistency. If you yap every day with a short take on what matters, you will build authority in 90 days. Your next product launch already has an audience waiting for it.

**Your Current State:** Zaal sees seven things a day, thinks "this is interesting because Y," and keeps thinking. He does not yap about the thought. The gap is output-frequency and systematization, not input-quality.

**The Multiplier:** If you commit to 7 minutes a day of curation (60-second video, three short posts, one podcast transcript yap), by the end of 90 days you will have a backlog of content that serves your next five product launches. Each launch is not a surprise to your audience—it is a natural extension of the lane you have been curating in.

### Module 2 Shippable Project

**Task: 7-Day Curation Sprint + Swipe File**

**Pick Your Lane:** The niche you will curate in for the next 7 days. This should be specific enough to own. Examples:

- AI agents for music creators (not just "AI for musicians")
- Onchain event infrastructure (not just "web3 events")
- Community tooling that actually works (not just "DAOs")
- Event economics and retention (not just "events")

Pick one. Write it down. **Your lane is also your zone of genius for ZAO.** You are not curating "AI trends for normal people." You are curating for your niche: builders, creators, organizers, community people who care about music, events, and web3.

**Structure (Monday-Sunday):**

Each day:
1. Find one thing in your lane that matters. This takes 20 minutes: scroll Farcaster casts, X posts, Reddit, YouTube, podcasts, research docs, product launches, pricing updates, policy changes. Find one thing that makes you stop and think "this is important because X."
2. Record a 60-second short-form video (TikTok, YouTube Shorts, Farcaster video, or Instagram Reel). Use this structure:

   "I saw this [thing]. Most people think it means [standard interpretation]. I think it actually means [your take]. Here's the move: [one action someone should take this weekend]."

   Example: "I saw OpenAI just changed the pricing for GPT-4o. Most people think this means video AI is now cheap. I think it actually means you should rethink your entire agent architecture because local models are now more cost-competitive than cloud. Here's the move: run your daily tasks on Ollama this weekend and time it. If it's under 2 seconds, you just freed up 20% of your API spend."

3. Post it. Post the video on Farcaster, X, TikTok, or YouTube Shorts. Do not cross-post to all platforms. Pick the one platform your niche lives on. For ZAO, that is probably Farcaster + X + occasional YouTube.

**Parallel Track (Wednesday):** Build your Swipe File.

As you find your daily things, also save examples of posts, videos, headlines, or yaps that work. Keep them in a Notion or Google Doc called "[Lane] Swipe File". Write one line about why each example works (curiosity, authority, fear, story, surprise, status). By day 7, you will have 15-20 examples of what resonates in your lane.

**Sunday Deliverable:** Seven videos published (one per day) + Swipe File with 15+ examples you love + one reflection on which of your 7 videos resonated most and why.

### Daily Practice (Week 2+)

After the 7-day sprint, continue this for 90 days:
- **Daily:** 20 minutes to find one thing + 5 minutes to record + 5 minutes to edit and post. (30 minutes total, front-loaded if possible.)
- **Weekly (Sunday):** Review the week. Which post got the most honest engagement? Which one did someone screenshot and send to a friend? Use that as a signal for next week's lane. Adjust if needed.

The loop: find signal, yap about it, measure engagement, adjust and repeat.

2. **[EXAMPLE CURATOR]** Linus Tech Tips on YouTube (YouTube.com/linustechtips). He curates the week in tech hardware. Watch 3 of his short-form videos. Note the structure: he found something, he explains why it matters, he tells you what to do about it. Fast, opinionated, useful. That is the model.

## Module 3: Agent Setup, Management & Local AI (Week 3)

### The Skill in Two Sentences

You design small AI agents that have context (files, calendar, task queue, past decisions), tools (API calls, database queries, sending messages), permissions (approval gates, human sign-off), and memory (it learns from past mistakes). This skill teaches you the architecture of ZOE and why "Agent as Operating System" is the future of work for one person.

### Why This Skill Is Durable

Prompt engineering (typing good questions into ChatGPT) is now table stakes. Everyone can do it. The next layer is designing systems. Companies will have 20 AI tools, 50 half-working workflows, and nobody knows how they talk to each other. The person who designs these systems (choosing what context each agent needs, where approval gates go, how memory persists, which jobs run locally vs cloud) becomes hard to replace. Prompt engineering is a day job. Agent architecture is a superpower.

### Why It Matters for You and ZAO

ZOE is your operating system. It has context, tools, permissions, memory. The gap: most of these are implicit or scattered. This module teaches you to make them explicit. You will design a mini "daily briefing agent for Zaal" that pulls (1) calendar, (2) task queue, (3) three information sources, and (4) one approval gate before acting. This teaches you context-retrieval-tool-use-permissions in one small project. You then fold this pattern into ZOE.

**Your Current State:** ZOE exists (doc 759). Hermes (coder/critic) is a prototype. The gap: formalizing which jobs should run local vs cloud, how memory persists across runs, where humans approve before acting, and which tools are sandboxed.

**The Multiplier:** If you systematize agent design, the next capability you add to ZOE (new critic role, memory block, tool permission, local-model routing) will ship in half the time because you have the architecture language. You will not be asking "how does this work?" You will be asking "where in the system does this fit?"

### Module 3 Shippable Project

**Task: Build a "Daily Briefing Agent" for Zaal**

This is a real project. You ship this as a feature or as a standalone tool. It lives in ZOE or next to ZOE.

**The Agent Does This:**

Every morning (or on demand), the agent:
1. Pulls your calendar for the day (via Supabase or Google Calendar API).
2. Pulls your task queue (from memory.md, a todo file, or Supabase).
3. Fetches three information sources (e.g., Farcaster posts from 5 followers, Bonfire recent episodes, ZAO Respect token price).
4. Summarizes: "Your day has three meetings: X, Y, Z. You have 4 follow-ups pending. The signal in your world right now: A, B, C."
5. Asks for approval: "Before I send this to Telegram, should I include the token price? Should I add a note about meeting Z?"
6. Sends the brief to Telegram or logs it to a file.

**Step 1 (Monday):** Design the Agent. Answer:

- What is the ONE job this agent should do? (Tomorrow morning, summarize what Zaal should know.)
- What context does it need? (Calendar, tasks, sources, past decisions.)
- What tools does it use? (API to fetch calendar, Supabase read query, Farcaster API, memory read.)
- What is the approval gate? (Show summary, ask Zaal via Telegram "look good?", wait for thumbs-up before sending.)
- What is success? (Agent takes 5 minutes to run, saves Zaal 15 minutes of reading, catches something he would have missed.)

Write these in a one-page design doc.

**Step 2 (Tuesday/Wednesday):** Code the Agent. Use LLM as your copilot (Claude via API, Claude Code, or Cursor). Build the smallest version:

- Fetch calendar (Google Calendar API or hardcode 3 meetings).
- Fetch task queue (read a notes file).
- Fetch one source (hit Bonfire API or hardcode 3 recent episodes).
- Write a summary.
- Log to console or Telegram.

Do NOT build the approval gate yet. Just ship v1 ugly.

**Step 3 (Thursday):** Test and Add Approval Gate.

- Run the agent for three days. Does it work? Does it save time?
- Add the approval gate: send the summary to Telegram, wait for a thumbs-up emoji (or ask for edits in plain text).
- Only then send it onward.

**Step 4 (Friday/Saturday):** Ship It.

- Deploy as a scheduled job (Vercel cron, GitHub Actions, or a Telegram command).
- Run it live for 48 hours.
- Measure: did it save you time? Did it catch something?

**Step 5 (Sunday):** Reflect and Plan.

- What worked? (Did the calendar pull work? Did the summary make sense?)
- What failed? (Did the Farcaster API rate-limit? Did the approval gate feel clunky?)
- What is v2? (Add local-model routing? Add a second source? Wire it deeper into ZOE?)

**Sunday Deliverable:** One-page reflection + live agent running + measurement of time saved and things caught.

### Daily Practice (Week 3)

- **Monday/Tuesday:** Design and research. 1-2 hours.
- **Wednesday/Thursday:** Code. 2-3 hours.
- **Friday/Saturday:** Test and ship. 2-3 hours.
- **Sunday:** Reflect and plan next. 1 hour.

This is heavy coding week. But you are building something real and shipping it.

### Real Resources

1. **[FULL]** Doc 759 (ZOE Orchestrator Architecture). Read this twice this week. Understand the current shape. Your daily briefing agent should either live inside ZOE or plug into it as a capability.

2. **[FULL]** Doc 928 (Agent Loop Best Practices). Read section on "Ground truth over confidence" and "Read state before acting". Your daily briefing agent should validate that its data is fresh before acting. This doc teaches why.

3. **[CODE EXAMPLES]** Vercel's AI SDK (sdk.vercel.ai/docs). Look at the tool-calling examples. See how to structure tool calls, handle results, and route based on permissions. Your agent uses tools. This SDK teaches the pattern.

4. **[TOOL]** Ollama (ollama.ai). If your agent needs to run locally (sensitive calendar data, cost savings), Ollama lets you run small LLMs on your machine. Download it. Run `ollama pull mistral` or `ollama pull neural-chat`. Play with it for 30 minutes. Understand the latency and quality. This teaches when to use local vs cloud.

### How You'll Know It Worked

- The agent runs and produces output (even if ugly).
- It saved you at least 10 minutes one day.
- It caught or summarized something you would have missed.
- You understand why context, tools, permissions, and memory matter for agents.
- You know where to wire it into ZOE.

## Module 4: Builder Distributors (Week 4)

### The Skill in Two Sentences

You ship the smallest working version of an idea (a script, a form, a feature, an event) and immediately get it in front of people. You watch where they get confused, you change the product, you change the story, you iterate. The loop between building and distributing is tight (48 hours).

### Why This Skill Is Durable

For decades, the split was clean: builders build in private, marketers sell in public. One person completes the loop themselves became increasingly rare. Now, with AI, one person can prototype, design, code, write copy, make videos, and iterate based on feedback—all in 48 hours. The person who does not separate the product from the market has the advantage: feedback lands in code faster, product decisions are data-driven instead of guessed, and the market tells you what matters before you spend two months on something nobody wants.

### Why It Matters for You and ZAO

You already do this. ZABAL Games shipped, got feedback, iterated. ZAOstock shipped as a dashboard, got feedback, added features. The gap: formalizing the loop and making it visible. If you document the "48-hour loop" (ship + 10 distribution pieces + measure reaction + iterate), you teach it to your teams. You also catch whether distribution-first changes product scope (spoiler: it does).

**Your Current State:** You ship intuitively and iterate fast. ZABAL Games 3-month hackathon is the model. The gap: no written playbook. No "here is the loop for a ZAO team to follow."

**The Multiplier:** If you write the loop down and teach it to Iman, Jose, others, you have a replicable pattern for shipping products. You also spot which distribution channels matter before you build (it changes what you build).

### Module 4 Shippable Project

**Task: Formalize the 48-Hour Loop on Your Lab Product**

You are shipping your lab product this week (or a real-world version of it). Document the full loop:

**Step 1 (Monday):** Prototype.

- Smallest working version. Ugly is fine.
- Feature-complete enough that someone can try it and say "yes, I see what this is" or "no, this is not for me."
- Could be code, could be a demo, could be a workflow, could be an event run.

**Step 2 (Monday-Tuesday):** Create 10 Distribution Pieces.

Before you announce that the thing exists, create:
1. One 60-90 second demo video (phone recording, screen recording, event clip).
2. Three short posts (Twitter/Farcaster, <280 chars or one paragraph).
3. One longer thread (3-5 tweets) explaining the "why" behind the product.
4. Two DMs (template message to send to 10 people who have the problem).
5. One landing page or doc (3-4 paragraphs explaining what it is, who it is for, how to try it).

These go out BEFORE or AT launch, not after. The idea: you do not ship in public and THEN tell people. You position the thing while you ship.

**Step 3 (Wednesday):** Launch and Measure.

- Post the demo video.
- Post the thread.
- Send DMs to 10 people who have the problem.
- Watch for 48 hours: replies, screenshots, forwards, sign-ups, actual usage.
- Measure: how many people replied? How many tried it? Which distribution piece drove the most people? Which one drove the RIGHT people (not just volume, but people who actually used the thing)?

**Step 4 (Thursday/Friday):** Iterate.

- What did people get confused about? Change the story or the product.
- Which distribution piece did NOT work? Drop it.
- Which one worked? Do more of it.
- Ship v1.1 with the feedback built in.

## Module 5: IRL Community Builders (Week 5)

### The Skill in Two Sentences

You host a real gathering (dinner, workshop, event, walk) around one sharp question. After, you send a recap that captures the best quotes, inside jokes, ideas, and one action per person. The recap turns the room into a network asset (media, recruiting, deal flow, trust).

### Why This Skill Is Durable

AI makes content abundant and advice abundant. What becomes scarce is trust and belonging. The person who can gather people around a question, capture what happens, and turn it into a network (not just a one-off event) becomes someone everyone wants to know. Real rooms become more valuable, not less, as everything else moves online. Recaps are the bridge between a single event and a lasting network.

### Why It Matters for You and ZAO

This is your superpower. You run ZABAL Games workshops, POIDH, Magnetiq events, ZAOstock. The gap: recaps are not systematized. Right now, attendees leave and the connection fades. If you send a recap within 48 hours with the best quotes, inside jokes, and one action each person should do, two things happen: (1) people forward it (it becomes media), (2) next invitation is easier (they trust the gathering because they felt seen in the recap).

**Your Current State:** You run great rooms. Events hit. People leave pumped. The gap: no recap process. The network potential is left on the table.

**The Multiplier:** If you systematize recaps, ZABAL Games becomes not just a workshop but a network. Attendees become collaborators. Recaps become recruiting assets (people send them to friends saying "you should know these people"). The room itself becomes a moat: people want to be in a space that matters enough to get a real recap.

### Module 5 Shippable Project

**Task: Host One Real Gathering and Capture a Recap**

This week you host one real gathering (or attend one and document it):

**Monday-Wednesday:** Plan.

- Who: 6-8 people you want to know better. Mix. Maybe 2 collaborators, 2 new people, 2 users of your product, 2 advisors or mentors.
- Where: Something real. Dinner. Walk. Breakfast. Hike. Not a Zoom.
- Question: One sharp question that makes people think. Examples: "What skill are you learning because of AI?", "What is the biggest problem in your space that nobody is solving?", "What product would you build if you had zero constraints?"
- Duration: 90 minutes to 2 hours.

Invite 8 people. Expect 6 to show.

**Thursday:** Host.

- Sit in a circle (not presentation mode).
- Ask the question. Let it breathe. Do not jump in.
- Take notes. One note per person on what they said. One note on the best quote or insight.
- Notice the inside jokes or funny moments.
- Listen for problems or ideas.

**Friday/Saturday:** Write the Recap.

- One page. Prose. Not a bullet list.
- The question.
- Best 3-5 quotes (with names).
- One inside joke or funny moment.
- Key ideas that came up.
- One action each person should do (or a collective action).

Examples:

"Maria said 'most agents are just fancy autocomplete'. That landed. Here is what I think she meant... Zaal, you mentioned building agent memory differently. Here is one thing I think you should research this week... Jose, you said you wish you had time to curate. Here is an idea: what if you recorded one 60-second take per day..."

The recap is personal and specific. Not generic.

**Sunday:** Send and Measure.

- Send the recap to all 6 attendees within 48 hours of the event.
- Watch for 48 hours: do people forward it? Do they send it to other people? Do they reply with adds or corrections?
- Measure: did it feel valuable enough to share?

**Deliverable (Sunday):** One-page recap + screenshot of sends + notes on engagement (forwards, replies, adds).

### Daily Practice (Week 5+)

- Host one real gathering per month minimum. More is better.
- Send a recap within 48 hours.
- Notice which recaps get forwarded. Adjust your question or your gathering format based on what resonates.

This becomes a rhythm: host > capture > share > measure > next gathering better.

## Module 6: Robotics Engineers (Week 6)

### The Skill in Two Sentences

You understand how to design, prototype, and source physical products. You learn the language of manufacturing (lead times, MOQ, shipping terms). You integrate software + hardware + supply chain into one product. This skill teaches humility and practical integration thinking.

### Why This Skill Is Durable

For 20 years, money went to people who moved pixels. The next 20 will reward people who move atoms too. The skill is not "build a robot." It is "design something that can actually be made, shipped, repaired, and used by a normal person." As AI makes software abundant, the constraint moves to the physical world. The person who can connect open source, manufacturing, and distribution has leverage most software people do not.

### Why It Matters for You and ZAO

Lower priority than the first five skills, but important for atoms: POIDH is an onchain physical event. WaveWarZ might have physical releases or merch. ZABAL Games has workshop materials or potential merch. When you build physical things, supplier language matters. You need to know MOQ (minimum order quantity), lead times, shipping terms, sampling costs. This prevents you from designing something that cannot actually be made.

**Your Current State:** You understand musicians and events. Hardware integration is orthogonal to your stack. However, the INTEGRATION insight applies: build + wire software + source manufacturing + validate.

**The Multiplier:** If you learn the language of suppliers and the realities of manufacturing, your next physical product (merch, event toolkit, limited release) will ship faster because you designed with constraints in mind from day one.

### Module 6 Shippable Project

**Task: Design a Small Physical Product (or Source One for ZAO)**

You do not need to build a robot. You do need to pick a real physical problem and solve it with the same integration mindset.

Examples for ZAO:
- ZABAL Games workshop toolkit (printed materials, a badge, a template kit organizers can run).
- POIDH event merch (limited-edition pins, stickers, or a physical recording/poster).
- WaveWarZ limited vinyl release or alt-token holder.
- ZAOstock physical badge or certificate.

Pick one. This is your physical project for the week.

**Step 1 (Monday):** Research and Design.

- What is the physical thing? (e.g., a printed toolkit for ZABAL organizers, a badge, a limited vinyl release).
- Who makes it? (Alibaba, PrintNinja, local manufacturer, open source hardware store).
- What are the specs? (Size, color, material, packaging).

Research 3 manufacturers. Get a quote for each. Note:
- MOQ (how many pieces minimum can you order?).
- Lead time (how long to produce and ship?).
- Unit cost (at MOQ, what is the cost per piece?).
- Sampling cost (can you get one sample to test?).

**Step 2 (Tuesday/Wednesday):** Request Samples.

Email one manufacturer: "I want to order [your product], MOQ is [your MOQ], delivery is [your timeline]. Can you send me one sample with specs and pricing?" Ask for CAD files, material specs, and shipping terms.

This is real supplier language. You are learning to talk to makers.

**Step 3 (Thursday):** Evaluate Sample (or Design).

- If you got a sample, test it. Does it work? Is it cheap/durable/cool enough?
- If not, design the specs in detail: dimensions, colors, packaging, branding.
- Think through the user experience: What does someone do with this when they get it? Does it serve that purpose?

**Step 4 (Friday/Saturday):** Plan the Production.

- Decide: will you make this for ZAO? (pilot or full run?)
- If yes: place order for 50-200 pieces (enough for a real test).
- If no: document why (cost, time, not the right time) and plan for next quarter.

**Step 5 (Sunday):** Reflect.

- What did you learn about manufacturing?
- What surprised you about sourcing and supply chain?
- When will this ship? (even if delayed to next quarter, know the plan).

**Deliverable (Sunday):** One-page doc with: physical product description, 3 manufacturer quotes, sample photos (if obtained), decision (make now or queue for later), production plan.

### Daily Practice (Week 6+)

- When you ship any physical thing, document supplier interaction.
- Talk to makers in their language (MOQ, lead times, tooling, sourcing).
- Think of every physical product as a software + supply-chain integration problem.

This becomes a reflex: before you design a physical product, you already know what it costs and how long it takes.

### Real Resources

1. **[FULL]** Doc 1008, Robotics Engineers section. Read the supplier language part specifically. Alibaba, CAD files, replacement parts, lead times. This is the vocabulary.

2. **[TOOL]** Alibaba.com. Spend 30 minutes searching for small manufacturing (badges, prints, vinyl, merch). Note the MOQ and lead times. Search for "custom badges", "printed packaging", "enamel pins". Learn the supplier lingo.

3. **[RESOURCE]** Hugging Face LaRobot (huggingface.co/lerobot). If you ever want to build a robot, this is the open source foundation. For now, just know it exists and shows what is possible with low-cost parts + AI.

### How You'll Know It Worked

- You talked to at least one real manufacturer.
- You requested a sample or got a quote.

- Next time you design something physical, you know what questions to ask and how to budget time + money.
