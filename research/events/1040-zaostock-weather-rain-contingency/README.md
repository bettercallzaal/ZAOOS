# 1040 — ZAOstock Weather Contingency: The Decision Doc 1032 Never Made

> **Goal:** Doc 1032 already gives ZAOstock a real mid-show weather PAUSE protocol (wind/lightning thresholds, who calls it, evacuation steps). What's never existed is the decision one level up: what happens if it's raining steadily all day, or forecast to be, BEFORE the show even starts. This doc closes that gap with a real policy, a real decision timeline, and real Ellsworth-specific weather data.

## Key Decisions

| Recommendation | Why |
|---|---|
| **Adopt "rain or shine" as ZAOstock's stated policy, not a rain date** | Real Maine precedent splits roughly evenly between "rain or shine" and rain-date deferral at this scale, but rain dates only work when there's genuine flexibility to reschedule - ZAOstock is locked to Oct 3 (part of the 9th Annual Art of Ellsworth / statewide Maine Craft Weekend, a fixed date nobody controls). A rain date isn't realistically available here, so "rain or shine" is the only policy that's actually honest to commit to |
| **State the rain-or-shine policy publicly, on the site, before anyone asks** | Every real Maine example found (Maine Made Crafts, Portland Fine Craft Show, Maine Whoopie Pie Festival, Arts in the Park Belfast) states its weather policy directly in event marketing, not as an FAQ answer. Zaostock.com currently says nothing - attendees have no way to know whether to expect a cancellation email if it drizzles |
| **Set a real go/no-go decision timeline: 7 days out (first check), 3 days out (confirm), 24 hours out (final call, if a call is even needed)** | This is the closest thing to an industry-standard cadence found in sourced event-safety guidance. Given "rain or shine" is the adopted policy, the 24-hour call is really only for genuinely dangerous conditions (lightning, high wind, flooding) - which is where doc 1032's existing pause protocol already picks up. This timeline is about not being caught flat-footed, not about deciding whether to cancel a drizzle |
| **The Site Lead makes the go/no-go call, same person doc 1032 already named for the mid-show pause decision** | Doc 1032 already established the Site Lead as the weather decision-maker mid-show (per its Finding 3 decision tree). Extending that same authority to the pre-show go/no-go call keeps one person accountable for weather decisions end-to-end, rather than splitting it across two different roles |
| **Don't sell "peak fall color" or "guaranteed sunny" - Ellsworth's own October numbers don't support it** | Ellsworth's October averages a 34% daily rain chance and is the area's wettest month (34). This isn't a reason to panic - "rain or shine" absorbs it - but it's a reason not to write marketing copy that implies otherwise |

## Findings

### 1. "Rain or shine" is the dominant policy at ZAOstock's exact scale, and a rain date isn't realistically available here

Real, sourced Maine examples at comparable scale (free or low-cost, single-day, outdoor, under 1,000 attendees) split into two groups:

**Rain or shine (event proceeds regardless, absent genuinely dangerous conditions):** Maine Made Crafts, Portland Fine Craft Show (Lincoln Park), Maine Whoopie Pie Festival, Arts in the Park (Belfast), Kennebunk First Parish Blueberry Fair, and the main program of the Machias Wild Blueberry Festival (its one indoor-contingency element is a single concert moved to a nearby church, not the whole festival).

**Rain date (event shifts to a second day):** Saco Sidewalk Arts Festival ("rain date: the following Sunday"), Gray Wild Blueberry Festival ("if severe weather is predicted for Saturday, the festival will be held on Sunday").

The deciding factor in which group an event falls into isn't scale - it's whether the event actually has flexibility to move. ZAOstock doesn't: Oct 3 is fixed as part of the 9th Annual Art of Ellsworth, itself inside the statewide Maine Craft Weekend (per doc 1031) - a date ZAOstock doesn't control and can't unilaterally shift a day later without falling outside that umbrella. That makes "rain or shine" the only policy that's actually available, not just the more common one. [FULL - direct research this session, 6+ named Maine event examples with sourced policies]

### 2. A real decision timeline exists in event-safety literature - 7 days / 3 days / 24 hours

Sourced event-safety guidance converges on a three-checkpoint cadence: a preliminary weather assessment ~7 days out, a confirmation check ~3-4 days out (when forecasts get meaningfully more accurate), and a final go/no-go call ~24 hours before the event. Given ZAOstock's adopted "rain or shine" policy, this timeline isn't really about deciding whether to hold the event - it's about not being surprised. The 24-hour checkpoint is where doc 1032's existing thresholds (wind 25mph+, lightning within 6-10 miles) actually matter: if the forecast already shows dangerous conditions 24 hours out, the Site Lead has a day's notice to line up contingencies (an indoor fallback for specific moments, earlier/later timing, extra tarping) rather than making that call live at 11:30am doc 1032's go/no-go checkpoint. [FULL - NEEFUSA volunteer-event safety guidance, cross-referenced against doc 1032's existing same-day thresholds]

### 3. Real "rain or shine" messaging is short and stated up front, not buried

Every sourced Maine example states its weather policy directly in the event listing, usually in the first line or in the date/time block itself - "Saturday June 27, 9-4PM, rain or shine" (Maine Made Crafts), "Rain or Shine" in caps as part of the headline (Maine Whoopie Pie Festival). None of them hedge it or bury it in an FAQ. Industry guidance backs this up explicitly: attendees should know the weather policy "before guests purchase tickets" (or, for a free event, before they make plans to come) so expectations are set, not managed after the fact. zaostock.com currently has zero weather-policy language anywhere on the site. [FULL - direct source review, 5 real Maine event listings]

### 4. For a free festival, cancellation has a real reputational cost even without refund liability

Doc 1032 already established doors are free (no refund risk). But sourced research on festival weather-cancellation fallout shows the cost isn't financial refunds - it's community trust. One documented case (ROMP Festival) cancelled a final day for weather and retained goodwill specifically because organizers communicated the decision clearly and attendees felt the call was sound, not arbitrary. The lesson for ZAOstock: since "rain or shine" is the policy, an actual cancellation should be rare enough that when it happens, it's unambiguous (real lightning risk, not "it's raining and turnout might be light") - a cancellation for a condition the stated policy claims to weather would read as flaky, not careful. [FULL - direct case study + event-cancellation-communication literature]

### 5. Ellsworth's real October numbers, for realistic planning (not marketing copy)

Ellsworth averages a **34% chance of rain on any given October day**, with October being the area's **wettest month** (~5 inches / 155mm average). When it does rain, the average rainy day sees about 0.56 inches. This is consistent with the "roughly 1-in-3 chance of rain" figure doc 1033 already cited for the region. No source found gives Oct 3-specific historical data (only monthly averages) - a real NOAA/NCEI historical pull for that specific date isn't something this session could fetch, and would be the natural next step if a more precise number is wanted closer to the date. [FULL - AccuWeather, Wanderlog, cross-checked against doc 1033's existing figure - consistent, not contradictory]

### 6. Communication plan for an actual cancellation, if it ever comes to that

Sourced guidance is consistent: notify internal staff/volunteers first, then vendors/performers, then the general public - all within 24 hours of the decision, across email, social, and any text/push channel available. Doc 1032 already has a Command Post + radio/runner system for day-of communication; that same chain is the right one to use for a pre-show cancellation call too, just triggered a day earlier than the day-of protocol assumes.
