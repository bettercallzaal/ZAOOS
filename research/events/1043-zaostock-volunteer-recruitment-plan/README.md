# 1043 — ZAOstock Volunteer Recruitment Plan: Filling Doc 1032's 20-26 Person Roster

> **Goal:** Doc 1032 needs 20-26 volunteers across three shift crews (load-in 6am-12pm, event-run 12pm-6pm, load-out 6pm-10:30pm). No plan has ever existed for how those people actually get recruited - just the headcount target. Doc 1007/1035 have repeatedly flagged the team as "hard to come by." This doc is the actual recruitment plan.

## Key Decisions

| Recommendation | Why |
|---|---|
| **Lead with Heart of Ellsworth's own volunteer network, not a cold recruitment campaign** | HoE already runs a volunteer-driven operation for its Thursday concert series and Holiday Parade, under Main Street America's Four Point Approach. This is the same relationship doc 1031 and doc 1042 already lean on for venue and press - extending it to volunteers is the highest-leverage single move available |
| **Recruit 28-31 people, not exactly 20-26** | Sourced no-show rates for volunteer events run 5-10% even when well-managed, up to 30-40% when poorly communicated. A 15-20% buffer over doc 1032's target headcount is the standard hedge against day-of gaps, not overkill |
| **Start recruitment now (July), not closer to the date** | Recruitment timelines for comparable events run 3-5 months out, with rosters finalizing 2-3 weeks before. Given the confirmed Oct 3 date, July is already the realistic start, not early |
| **One Volunteer Coordinator, not a coordinator-per-shift structure** | At 28-31 total volunteers, sourced guidance treats a single coordinator as manageable; splitting the role by shift adds coordination overhead a first-year event doesn't need. One informal "shift buddy" per crew (not a formal lead) is the lighter-weight alternative to a 3-coordinator structure |
| **Treat Year 1 as the hard year, deliberately** | Every sourced Maine example (Maine Lobster Festival's 1,300+ volunteers, Yarmouth Clam Festival's multi-decade base) started from zero. The realistic goal for 2026 isn't an easy recruitment - it's building the base that makes 2027 meaningfully easier through returning volunteers and referrals |

## Findings

### 1. Recruitment channels, ranked by realistic leverage for a first-year event

- **Heart of Ellsworth's existing volunteer network** - the single highest-leverage channel. HoE already coordinates volunteers for its own recurring programs; a direct ask to fold ZAOstock into that existing base beats building one from nothing.
- **Local Facebook community groups + Nextdoor** - sourced as high-conversion, low-cost channels specifically because posts read as community invitations, not corporate recruitment. A small paid boost ($50-200) is a realistic, cheap amplifier.
- **College of the Atlantic** (Bar Harbor, ~18 miles from Ellsworth) - many college volunteer/service programs count community-service hours toward degree or internship credit; worth a direct outreach to their Career Development office, though this session's research couldn't confirm ZAOstock-specific fit beyond general practice.
- **Local high school community-service-hour programs** - Maine high schools commonly accept event volunteering toward required service hours; Maine's VolunteerME platform is the standard way to reach them.
- **VolunteerMatch/Idealist** - free to post for a registered nonprofit, best suited for a one-time event listing rather than ongoing recruitment.

### 2. Timeline: recruitment should already be starting, with a hard finalize point in mid-September

Sourced timeline for an event this size: initial recruitment push starting ~3-5 months out (matches now, July, for an Oct 3 event), first-wave confirmations through July-August, roster finalized by mid-September, detailed shift info out ~2 weeks before, and an in-person or virtual orientation in the final week. This lines up cleanly with doc 1032's own Sep-level planning cadence and the Aug 15 dry-run question doc 1035 already flagged as still undecided. [FULL - per subagent research]

### 3. No-show buffer: recruit 28-31, not exactly 20-26

Sourced no-show rates for volunteer events run 5-10% when well-managed (clear confirmations, reminders, detailed pre-event info) and 30-40% when communication is weak. A 15-20% recruitment buffer over doc 1032's 20-26 target - landing at roughly 28-31 people recruited - is the standard hedge, not padding. Confirmation reminders at 3-4 days out and again day-of, plus a volunteer-management tool (even something as simple as a shared signup sheet with automated reminders) meaningfully reduce no-shows. [FULL - per subagent research]

### 4. Incentives: the crew shirt already planned is the right instinct - pair it with food, not more

ZAOstock's `/apply` page already lists a crew shirt as a stated volunteer perk - sourced research confirms this is exactly the right kind of incentive at this scale (Maine Lobster Festival pairs its shirt with a discount on food; the free-entry incentive common at ticketed festivals is moot here since ZAOstock is already free). The addition worth making: meal vouchers or provided food/water during the shift, plus a visible on-stage or social thank-you - a proven, low-cost incentive pairing that doesn't require inventing anything new. [FULL - per subagent research, cross-referenced against the live `/apply` page's existing crew-shirt perk]

### 5. Structure: one Volunteer Coordinator is enough at this scale

For a 28-31 person total volunteer base across 3 shifts, sourced guidance treats a single coordinator role as sufficient - recruitment, confirmations, shift scheduling, day-of check-in, and post-event thank-yous, using a lightweight tool (a shared form or simple signup platform, not necessarily paid software). Splitting into a coordinator-per-shift model is real practice at larger events but adds a coordination layer this size doesn't need yet. An informal "shift buddy" - an experienced volunteer other volunteers can ask questions of - is the lighter-weight version of a shift lead. [FULL - per subagent research]

### 6. Real Maine precedent: every big volunteer base started at zero

Maine Lobster Festival now runs on 1,300+ volunteers annually and Yarmouth Clam Festival (running since 1965) draws hundreds - both are useful proof that Maine communities do turn out for local festivals, but neither started large. The honest read for ZAOstock: 2026 is genuinely the hardest year to recruit for, precisely because there's no returning-volunteer base or word-of-mouth yet. Treating this year's volunteers exceptionally well (the incentive pairing in Finding 4, a genuine thank-you per doc 1041's post-event plan) is what turns a hard first year into an easier second one.
