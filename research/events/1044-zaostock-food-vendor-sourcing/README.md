# 1044 — ZAOstock Food Vendor Sourcing: A Real Plan and a Hard Permit Deadline

> **Goal:** Doc 1032 already assumes food vendors exist - a 10:00am vendor load-in wave, a vendor zone sign, a vendor coordinator role - but nothing has ever addressed how vendors actually get recruited, what they're expected to provide, or what permits are legally required. This doc closes that gap and surfaces a real, dated deadline nobody has flagged before.

## Key Decisions

| Recommendation | Why |
|---|---|
| **Recruit local, not from a food-truck circuit** | Doc 1034's Ellsworth restaurant list (Provender Kitchen + Bar, Finn's Irish Pub, Union River Lobster Pot, Cleonice, Serendib, Fogtown Brewing, Airline Brewing) is a lower-friction vendor pipeline than cold-recruiting external food trucks - these businesses already have Ellsworth customer relationships, likely already hold valid Maine food-service licenses, and benefit directly from downtown foot traffic |
| **Target 1-2 vendors, not more** | Sourced guidance for a 500-person, single-stage, 6-hour festival treats 1 vendor per 400-500 attendees as standard, with 2 vendors (a savory + a dessert/beverage pairing) as the realistic ceiling. More than that oversupplies a crowd that isn't primarily there for food |
| **No booth fee in Year 1** | Charging vendors upfront adds friction for a first-year event with no proven attendance track record. Position participation as community/promotional value instead - the standard approach sourced for comparable free festivals in their first year |
| **The September 3, 2026 permit deadline is real and non-negotiable - flag it now, not in September** | Maine DHHS requires temporary food service license applications 30 days before the event for any vendor without an existing license. For an Oct 3 event, that's September 3. This is a real, dated, easy-to-miss requirement that didn't exist in any prior ZAOstock doc |
| **Verify vendor permits before allowing setup, in writing** | This is the event organizer's actual legal responsibility, not the vendor's alone - operating without a valid license exposes ZAOstock to liability, not just the vendor |

## Findings

### 1. Local restaurants are the realistic vendor pipeline, not a cold food-truck search

Doc 1034 already identified 8 real, currently-operating downtown Ellsworth restaurants. For a first-year, unproven event, direct outreach to these already-known businesses is lower-friction than recruiting external food trucks with no local ties - they already have Ellsworth customer bases, plausibly already hold Maine food-service licenses (removing the temporary-permit question entirely for them), and benefit from the same downtown foot-traffic argument doc 1031 already makes for sponsors. A backup channel (local Facebook groups, direct outreach to newer food trucks) exists if none of the known restaurants want a full pop-up. [FULL - per subagent research, cross-referenced directly against doc 1034's existing Ellsworth restaurant list]

### 2. Realistic vendor count and mix

Sourced industry guidance treats 1 food vendor per 400-500 attendees as standard, meaning **1-2 vendors** for ZAOstock's 500-person, 6-hour, single-stage format - not the 4-5 vendors that would make sense at a food-focused festival. Expected sales volume at this scale is realistically 10% of attendees (roughly 50 total purchases across 6 hours), which one source found explicitly caused a comparable event to conclude 5 vendors was oversupply. The recommended pairing: one savory main-course vendor plus one dessert/beverage vendor, covering different parts of the 6-hour window rather than competing for the same lunch-rush moment. [FULL - per subagent research]

### 3. Maine permit requirements - two real pathways, one hard deadline

Every food vendor needs either an **existing Maine food-service license** (if a local restaurant already holds one, no additional permit needed - just verify it's current) or a **Maine DHHS Temporary Food Service License** (Form HHE-602) for a vendor without one. The temporary license **must be submitted 30 days before the event** - for October 3, that's **September 3, 2026** - and DHHS will not process late applications. A vendor is capped at 3 temporary licenses per year; a vendor who's already worked 2-3 other 2026 events may not be eligible without upgrading to a mobile-unit license, worth asking about upfront. This 30-day deadline is a genuinely new fact - no prior ZAOstock research doc has surfaced it, and it's easy to miss since it's a state health-department process separate from the City of Ellsworth's own Aug 19 mass-gathering permit deadline. [FULL - per subagent research, citing Maine DHHS's own published forms and process]

### 4. Fee structure: skip the booth fee this year

Sourced guidance for first-year free festivals recommends no upfront vendor fee - charging one adds recruitment friction precisely when the event has no attendance track record to justify it. If ZAOstock becomes a recurring annual event (which doc 1031 already frames it as - "Year 1 of a multi-year festival"), a modest flat fee ($100-200) or hybrid model becomes reasonable once there's a real attendance number to point to. Year 1 should treat vendor participation as a mutual promotional exchange, not a revenue line. [FULL - per subagent research]

### 5. Recruitment timeline and what to ask each vendor for

Realistic timeline: begin outreach immediately (this doc's writing is already July), confirm 1-2 vendors by mid-August, hold the September 3 permit deadline as a hard internal flag, and get a final logistics call done by late September (space needs, setup time, water/power needs) ahead of doc 1032's existing 10:00am vendor load-in wave. Each vendor conversation should cover: current license status, expected price point/volume, space and power/water needs, and liability insurance status (per doc 1045's finding that vendors are typically expected to carry their own coverage and list ZAOstock as additional insured). [FULL - per subagent research, cross-referenced against doc 1032's existing load-in timeline]
