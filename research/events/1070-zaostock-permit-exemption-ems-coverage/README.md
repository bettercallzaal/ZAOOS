# 1070 - ZAOstock Permit Exemption: Probably Doesn't Apply, and EMS Is a Fire-Chief Call Inside the Same Process

> **Goal:** Doc 1035 carried two open Tier 2 items forward from docs 1031/1032: whether ZAOstock qualifies
> for Ordinance Chapter 14's city-sponsorship exemption (avoiding the 45-day notice + surety bond), and
> whether mutual-aid EMS coverage is available/required for Oct 3. Both turn out to run through the same
> process and the same Aug 19 deadline - this doc resolves them together with real contacts, not just
> flags them again.

## Key Decisions

| Recommendation | Why |
|---|---|
| **Plan for the standalone Mass Gathering License path, not the exemption - the exemption almost certainly doesn't apply** | The ordinance's exact text: events must be "sponsored and under the direct supervision of the City of Ellsworth" to qualify. Art of Ellsworth is organized by Heart of Ellsworth, an independent 501(c)(3) - the City is a sponsor/partner, not the direct organizer/permit-holder. A strict reading says ZAOstock, as a sub-event of a nonprofit-run umbrella, doesn't meet the bar - though this needs a direct call to confirm, not just this doc's reading |
| **Call the City this week, not closer to Aug 19** | The Aug 19, 2026 deadline (45 days before Oct 3) is a hard cutoff for filing with the Police Chief. If the exemption doesn't apply (likely), ZAOstock needs the corporate surety bond ($200-500) lined up too - that's not something to discover the exemption failed with only days left |
| **Don't assume EMS standby is bundled into the permit for free - it's a Fire Chief discretionary call, and it can cost real money** | Chapter 14 lets the Fire Chief require police/fire personnel "at the applicant's expense" as a condition of approval. For a 200-400 person event this size, standby probably isn't automatically mandated (comparable ordinances elsewhere in Maine trigger mandatory EMS at much higher headcounts), but if it IS required, real cost is $1,500-2,000+ for a 6-hour standby ambulance crew - a serious hit against the real ~$5K/~$1.5K-on-hand budget if it lands |
| **One phone call can answer both questions at once** | The permit contact (City Clerk or Police Chief) and the EMS-requirement decision (Fire Chief) are part of the same Mass Gathering License review - worth asking about both in the same conversation rather than two separate outreach threads |

## Findings

### 1. The exemption's actual text draws a sharper line than "part of Art of Ellsworth" suggests

Ordinance Chapter 14: "All outdoor events, festivals, music concerts, fairs and other large gatherings
**sponsored and under the direct supervision of the City of Ellsworth** including the School Department
shall be exempt from the provisions of this ordinance." Art of Ellsworth is curated and run by Heart of
Ellsworth (a Main Street America-accredited 501(c)(3) nonprofit, not a city department) - the City
co-sponsors and partners (a joint Community Events Calendar launched in 2026, several joint initiatives),
but Heart of Ellsworth is the organizer of record. The ordinance's exemption language doesn't mention
umbrella events or sub-events at all - it's written for events the City itself directly runs. **Best-effort
read: ZAOstock likely does NOT qualify**, though the ordinance text found is from a 2016 PDF and City
Council minutes from June 2026 reference Chapter 14 being proposed for repeal/replacement - worth asking
directly whether a newer version changes this. [FULL - ordinance PDF read directly, cross-referenced
against Heart of Ellsworth's own site and City Hall's public announcements]

### 2. EMS standby is a Fire Chief discretionary call bundled into the same permit review, not automatic

Chapter 14 lets the Fire Chief require fire/police personnel "as a condition of approval... at the
applicant's expense" - it doesn't specify a headcount threshold or mandate standby ambulance outright.
Comparable Maine ordinances (Town of Arundel's mass-gathering rule) put this same discretion in the
Selectmen's hands, scaled to the event's actual risk profile. Published event-medical literature backs
this up: ambulance transport rates from mass gatherings run lower than community baseline, and roughly
46% of reserved standby ambulances at events go unused - so a blanket standby requirement for 200-400
people isn't a given. **But it can still be required**, and if it is, real Northeast pricing runs
$1,500-2,000+ for a 6-hour single-ambulance standby (roughly $75/hour plus travel time). Ellsworth's own
coverage: Ellsworth Fire Department provides paramedic-level EMS directly; Northern Light Maine Coast
Hospital's Medical Transport handles ambulance transport; Hancock County Ambulance Service is the county
backup. [FULL - ordinance text, Maine comparable-ordinance research, event-medical cost literature, 12
sources checked]
