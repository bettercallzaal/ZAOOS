### Donation mechanics (three channels — ALL underexplained publicly)

1. **2% auto-reinvestment** — FarmDrop reinvests 2% of every sale into FFH automatically. Already active for every purchase.
2. **Round-up at checkout** — Customer can add $5, $10, $25, or $50 to any order, funds go directly to FFH.
3. **Buy-One-Donate-One pricing** — Select items priced "as close to the wholesale price of purchasing two of any given item" so checking out = buying one for yourself + donating one to a food-insecure neighbor.

## Pull-Quote Bank (for clips, captions, pull-quotes in articles)

### On farmer-customer connection
> "These farmers that you're buying from online are your farmers. They're your producers. They want to serve you and they want to know what you want."

> "I once requested Abrams Creamery do butifarra sausage because I missed butifarra. I grew up in Barcelona. The next week they had a butifarra sausage, and it's one of their highest-selling products. And that idea came from me as a customer. Now they're selling butifarra across all their wholesale customers."

### On zero-waste model
> "Customers are telling the farmers exactly what to bring to them. And there is zero waste. And there is so much variety, because there's unlimited space online for farmers to put their entire inventory."

### On FarmDrop vs. farmers' markets
> "A farmer can't do farmers' markets more than days in the week, even if they did a farmer's market every week. But they can drop off product and have it arrive to 13 different communities on the same day through a collaborative distribution model."

> "Farm Drop has a multiplier effect on farmers' ability to reach customers."

### On accessibility
> "Some customers just like buying their food online. Others can't drive, or are immunocompromised, or can't find the time, or work two jobs and need to have their food delivered. There's a necessities piece here for the customer."

### On pandemic
> "Many farmers still on Farm Drop today will tell you that Farm Drop saved their business during the pandemic. Point blank."

### On unit sizes
> "You can get 2 pounds of chard, you can get 5 pounds of coffee, you can get 50 pounds of chicken or a whole lamb. These are units that just don't exist in the grocery store or at the farmer's market."

### On dignity + food security
> "We can say to community food organizations with dignity: we need 25 pounds of carrots, and us say, absolutely, no problem. We'll deliver them on Wednesday."

### On food system variety
> "There needs to be support for more variety of ways in which customers access locally farmed, locally made, produced healthy fresh food."

> "The farmer's market and Farm Drop and the farm stand and the local food sections at Hannaford and the co-op — all be these shared spaces that are collaborating, working with a lot of the same farms."

### On community convening
> "What is important about building community food systems is not just placing orders online... but also the events that bring people together to gather, to see each other, to meet, to dance."

### On May 24 meaning
> "This event on May 24th is a really important turning point for Farm Drop for a variety of reasons."
